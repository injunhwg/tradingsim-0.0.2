# How to Play
A player’s objective in the Stock Trading Game is to amass the portfolio of stocks and cash with the greatest liquidation value.

All players start with the same portfolio of five shares of a stock valued @ $40/share, plus $200 cash, for a total portfolio of $400. They then buy and sell stocks based on public and private information about the company revealed to them over the course of the game. At the end, a player’s portfolio is valued at the underlying value of a company’s stock times the number of shares they own plus the cash they have.

# Rules

## Individual Play

- Each player starts with the same portfolio.
- Players may share information with other players.

## Buy and Sell Transactions

- Players may place limit or market orders.
- Players may hold positive or negative (short) positions.

## Credit Constraints

- Players can short up to 5 (negative) shares of the company;
- Players can borrow up to $200 @ 10% (negative cash);
- Players may sell shares (or spend cash) that they don’t own.

## Game End

- Liquidation values of the stock is revealed (the sum of each stock’s 10 cards);
- Market (Buy + Hold) value + return is computed;
- Player portfolio value + return + rank is computed;
- Game Leaderboard is presented.

## Ending/Liquidation Value of Stocks

The stock value is determined by the sum of 10 playing cards randomly drawn at the beginning of the game (automated and hidden from view). The value of each card varies according to the card’s numerical value and color (black or red). Below is a summary of how card values are determined:

- Cards 2-9 are face value
- Face cards (Jack, Queen, King) equal 10, Aces equal 20
- Black cards = Card Value x (2)
- Red cards = Card Value x (-1). Note that all red cards reduce the overall value of a stock since they are negative. 

Example: Assume that the stock is composed of the following ten cards.

- Black cards: J, 9,8,6,4 = 20+18+16+12+8 = 74
- Red cards: A, Q, J, 8, 2 = -20-10-10-8-2 = -50
- Thus, Orange’s liquidation value = 74 + (-50) = 24

## Getting and Using Information

Players gain information about the company through public sources: two Stock Analyst Reports (SARs), and Earnings per Share (EPS) reports released during the course of play. Players may also purchase private “peeks” of three randomly chosen active-card contributions from the company’s underlying stack. Players therefore have access to incomplete information about the company:

- Security Analyst Reports (SARs): Each report reveals the sum of five randomly chosen cards from a company’s stack + $20
- Earnings per Share (EPS): For the company, one card is deleted, one card is added, and the difference is reported
- Private Information: For $1, players may “peek” at 3 randomly selected active-card contributions. They may buy as many “peeks” as they like; each time the sample is chosen at random from the company’s active card stack.

The default game duration is 15 minutes. While private peeks can be purchased at any time during the game, public information is released on this schedule:

Time After Start	Default Time Remaining
- 1st Security Analyst Report (SAR)	0:00	15:00
- 1st EPS announcement	3:00	12:00
- 2nd EPS announcement	6:00	9:00
- 2nd SAR	7:30	7:30
- 3rd EPS announcement	9:00	6:00
- 4th EPS announcement	12:00	3:00
- Market Closes	15:00	0:00

## Trading

Players can buy, sell, or hold shares of stock through market orders, limit orders, bids, and asks. Players can also take out cash loans and hold negative positions (also known as “short selling”).

## End of Game

When time runs out, all stocks are liquidated and their true value revealed. A leaderboard shows who successfully beat the market, and who claimed the top spot!

Since liquidation values, earnings reports, and market trades are based upon randomly generated information, every Stock Trading Game unfolds differently. Players may experience an up market, a down market, or anything in between. Playing several rounds of the game affords students the opportunity to practice computing annualized returns.
